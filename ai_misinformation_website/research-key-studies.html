Key Studies

A curated set of studies that explain how misinformation is spread, measured, and challenged across social and technical systems.

Misinformation, Disinformation, and Fake News: Lessons from an Interdisciplinary, Systematic Literature Review

Author:

Broda et al., 2024

Excerpt:

The authors reviewed 1,261 journal articles published between 2010 and 2021. Most research is data-driven and focuses on how false information spreads, how it can be detected, and what it looks like. The review also identifies gaps in current research and suggests directions for future studies.

Multimodal analysis of disinformation and misinformation

Author:

Wilson et al., 2023

Excerpt:

Disinformation and misinformation in the media have drawn growing attention from researchers and policymakers. Since most communication involves more than just words, multimodal analysis is important to understand false information. The review brings these fields together and shows that research on multimodal disinformation has grown rapidly since early 2020.

Thinking clearly about misinformation

Author:

Tay et al., 2024

Excerpt:

The article argues that we need a clear way to solve the disagreement about misinformation. The authors believe misinformation does have real impacts, and it can be both a symptom and a cause of larger problems. They also suggest that researchers should consider different aspects of misinformation, such as its topic, type, and how widely it spreads.

The science of fake news

Author:

Lazer et al., 2018

Excerpt:

The rise of fake news shows that traditional protections against misinformation are weakening in the internet age. This article discusses existing research from social science and computer science on why people believe fake news and how it spreads. Fake news is not a new problem, but this article focuses on unanswered scientific questions related to its recent political forms.

Social media and the spread of misinformation: infectious and a threat to public health

Author:

Denniss et al., 2025

Excerpt:

Misinformation is a serious threat to public health and society. Social media helps spread it quickly around the world. Health misinformation can influence people to make bad decisions, like refusing vaccines, and can lower trust in official institutions. Many factors cause this problem, such as non-experts posting easily, bots, algorithms, and the lack of action from tech companies.

(Why) Is Misinformation a Problem?

Author:

Adams et al., 2023

Excerpt:

Research on misinformation has grown rapidly in the last ten years, but one key question is often ignored: why is misinformation actually a problem? The authors question the view that technology is the main cause of its spread and impact. These doubts suggest that efforts to fight misinformation may harm democratic norms.
