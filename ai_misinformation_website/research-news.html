Category 1 : Real-World Impact of Misinformation

Mayor warns of London ‘disinformation blizzard’

Sadiq Khan warns London faces a “dark blizzard of online disinformation” from foreign and far-right groups. Research shows false claims about crime and migration have risen sharply, though homicide rates are at a record low. The mayor calls for greater transparency from social media companies. Critics say he is trying to silence legitimate criticism.

CDC shooter believed COVID vaccine was harmful; union says misinformation put staff at risk

A Georgia man who blamed the COVID-19 vaccine for making him depressed and suicidal shot at the CDC headquarters in Atlanta, killing a police officer. The suspect, Patrick Joseph White, was armed with five guns and died at the scene. His father said White had become fixated on the COVID-19 vaccine. A union representing CDC workers called on federal officials to condemn vaccine misinformation, saying it puts scientists at risk and fuels violence.

Category 2 : AI-Generated Content and How to Identify It

Hospital alert after fake doctor-endorsed videos

A south London hospital trust has issued an alert after fake AI-generated videos falsely claimed its doctors endorse weight loss products. The trust said its clinicians would never promote such products. Experts say the videos are a scam designed to make money, using fake doctor profiles and followers from Vietnam.

This article explains how AI-generated content makes it harder to spot fake news, especially during breaking news like the Iran war. To identify AI content, look for visual clues, check the source, listen to experts, use detection tools, and slow down before sharing.

A quick guide to spotting misinformation

This article explains how to spot and stop misinformation online. It defines fake news and disinformation as false content created to mislead, while misinformation is false information shared without bad intent. AI makes it harder to know what to trust. Tips include checking multiple sources, verifying author credentials, and explaining why false claims are wrong. When talking to someone who believes misinformation, stay calm, listen, ask questions, and understand emotional factors like confirmation bias and herd mentality. The goal is to build critical thinking, not win arguments.

Category 3 : Platform Policies & Responsibility

YouTube to start bringing back accounts of creators banned for misinformation

YouTube will allow creators banned for COVID-19 and election misinformation to rejoin the platform, as those policies no longer exist. The company says the move supports free speech. It follows pressure from President Trump and conservatives who accused tech companies of unfairly silencing right-wing voices.

Category 4 : Misinformation During Breaking News

AI fuels false claims after Charlie Kirk’s death, CBS News analysis reveals

After the killing of activist Charlie Kirk, AI-powered misinformation spread rapidly on social media. X’s chatbot Grok misidentified the suspect, posted altered photos, and gave contradictory information. Other AI tools like Perplexity and Google’s AI Overview also produced false details. Experts say people trust AI too much, and Utah’s governor urged the public to ignore online misinformation.

Fact-checking Trump health rumors over Easter weekend

Over Easter weekend, false rumors spread on social media claiming President Trump was hospitalized. There is no evidence to support this. Users shared an old video from 2024 and an AI-generated image. The White House denied the rumors, and Trump appeared publicly on April 6. The article shows how outdated content and AI tools fuel misinformation.
